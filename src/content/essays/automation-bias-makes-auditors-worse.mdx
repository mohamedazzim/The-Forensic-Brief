## The Assumption

We assume that adding AI tools to an auditor's workflow makes them more accurate. The tool catches what the human misses. The human catches what the tool misses. Together, they're better than either alone.

This assumption is wrong.

## The Evidence

Research on automation bias — the tendency to defer to automated recommendations — shows a consistent pattern. When humans use AI-assisted audit tools, their accuracy doesn't improve. It gets worse.

The mechanism is simple. The AI tool generates a finding. The auditor reviews it. But the auditor's review is now influenced by the AI's suggestion. Instead of independently evaluating the evidence, the auditor anchors on the AI's conclusion and works backward to justify it.

This is called **confirmation bias amplified by automation**. The AI doesn't just assist — it reshapes the human's judgment.

## The Rubber Stamp Problem

In traditional auditing, the human auditor is the quality control mechanism. They bring skepticism, domain expertise, and independent judgment. When you add an AI tool that pre-filters, pre-analyzes, and pre-concludes, you've effectively removed the quality control mechanism you were trying to enhance.

The auditor becomes a rubber stamp. They review the AI's work not to catch errors but to confirm the AI's conclusions. This is worse than having no AI at all, because now there's an illusion of double-checking that isn't actually happening.

## What Should Change

1. **Separate the AI and human review stages** — The auditor should review evidence independently before seeing the AI's analysis
2. **Track override rates** — If an auditor never overrides the AI, that's a red flag, not a sign of quality
3. **Rotate AI tools** — Don't let auditors build familiarity with a single tool's biases
4. **Measure accuracy with and without AI** — The only way to know if AI helps is to periodically measure without it
